(11) Example of a Dynkyn system or \(\lambda\)-system \(\mathcal{L} \).
Given a state space \( \Omega \) and its \(\sigma \)-algebra \(\mathcal{F}\), a common \(\lambda\)-system \(\mathcal{L} \) is that set of events where two measures \(P,Q\) agree on. I.e. $$ \mathcal{L} = \{A \in \mathcal{F} \text{ | } P(A) = Q(A) \}. $$ Consider for instance two coin tosses. Then we have that the state space is $$ \Omega = \{HH,HT,TH,TT \}, $$ and the event space is $$ \mathcal{F} = \mathcal{P}^{\Omega} = \{ \{HH\},\{HT\},\{TH\},\{TT\},\{HH, HT\},\{HH, TH\},\{HH, TT\}, $$ $$ \{HT, TH\},\{HT, TT\},\{HT, TT\},\{HH, HT, TH\},\{HH, HT, TT\}, \{HH, TH, TT\},\{HT, TH, TT\}, \{HH,HT,TH,TT \} \} $$ Consider the measure \(P\) that creates the completely unbiased probability space $$ (\Omega, \mathcal{F},P), $$ where $$ P(\{HH\}) = P(\{HT\}) = P(\{TH\}) = P(\{TT\}) = 1/4. $$ (is it enough to give the probabilities of the events that generate the \(\sigma \)-algebra \(\mathcal{F}\)?)
And the measure \(Q\) that creates a probability space $$ (\Omega, \mathcal{F},Q) $$ where the second toss is magically always equal to the first toss: $$ Q(\{TH\}) = Q(\{HT\}) = 0; Q(\{HH\}) = Q(\{TT\}) = 1/2. $$ Then these two measures agree and have value of exactly 1/2 on each of the following events except the last two: $$\mathcal{L} = \{ \{HH, HT\},\{HH, TH\},\{HT, TT\}, \{HT, TT\}, \{HH,HT,TH,TT \}, \oslash \}.$$ This is a \(\lambda\)-system which is not a \(\sigma\)-algebra.
